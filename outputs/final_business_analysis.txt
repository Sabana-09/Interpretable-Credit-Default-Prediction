1. Executive Summary

This report provides a complete business-focused analysis of the credit default prediction model developed using the UCI Credit Card dataset. The model uses an XGBoost classifier integrated within a full preprocessing pipeline and validated with SHAP explainability techniques. The goal of this report is to explain how the model behaves from a real-world business perspective, including what drives its predictions, why it performs the way it does, where it succeeds, and where it fails. Special attention is placed on model interpretability, fairness considerations, and the potential impact on operational decision-making.

The model achieves an accuracy of 81.9%, a precision of 66.8%, a recall of 36.0%, and an ROCâ€“AUC of 77.9%. These metrics indicate the model is effective at identifying true non-defaulters but could be improved in identifying high-risk customers who will default. Global SHAP analysis shows that recent repayment history (PAY_0) is the strongest driver of risk, while features such as LIMIT_BAL and demographic indicators (like MARRIAGE) also influence predictions. Local SHAP explanations on three real customer cases (a True Positive, True Negative, and False Positive) offer deeper insights into individual-level decision-making.

Finally, the analysis discusses fairness implications, operational risks, and threshold tuning strategies to help businesses deploy the model responsibly and effectively.

2. Business Problem Understanding

Financial institutions face substantial risk from customer loan or credit-card defaults. Early identification of customers likely to default in the upcoming billing cycle enables the organization to:

Minimize financial losses

Trigger preventive actions (e.g., reminders, negotiation, restructuring)

Adjust credit limits or interest rates

Satisfy compliance expectations around risk modeling

The prediction must be explainable and fair because credit decisions are legally regulated and audited. A black-box model without transparency would not meet business or regulatory requirements.

This project addresses this need by building a transparent, well-evaluated model and using SHAP explainability to provide insights suitable for loan officers, risk managers, and regulators.

4. Model Performance and What It Means for the Business
4.1 Accuracy = 81.9%

This means the model is correct for 8 out of 10 customers.

4.2 Precision = 66.8%

If the model predicts a customer will default, it is correct two-thirds of the time.

Business meaning:
The organization can trust that most flagged customers are truly risky. This is good when actions based on predictions are costly (e.g., credit limit reduction).

4.3 Recall = 36.0%

8. Fairness and Ethical Considerations

Credit scoring systems are monitored closely by regulators.
Two fairness risks were identified:

8.1 Use of MARRIAGE as a Top Feature

Although SHAP reveals MARRIAGE has partial predictive value, this may lead to:

Disparate impact on unmarried individuals

Questions about demographic bias

Regulatory scrutiny

Recommendation:
Regularly monitor false positive/negative rates across demographic groups.

8.2 Over-Reliance on Past Delinquency

Many customers temporarily fall behind but recover.
Case 17778 shows how repeated delinquencies can dominate predictions even when the customer eventually pays.

Recommendation:
Incorporate additional features:

Income

Total assets

Employment stability

This reduces structural bias.

The credit default prediction model performs well and provides reliable, transparent insights into customer behavior. SHAP explainability reveals the key drivers of risk, and the combination of global and local insights provides a solid foundation for both operational decision-making and regulatory compliance.

While the model has strong precision and a robust ROCâ€“AUC, its recall can be improved with threshold tuning or by expanding the dataset. Ethical and fairness considerations must remain central to its deployment, especially when demographic features like MARRIAGE appear influential.
